Most résumés are read by a machine before a human ever sees them. That machine is an applicant tracking system (ATS), and understanding what it does turns résumé writing from guesswork into engineering.

## What an ATS actually does

An ATS ingests your file, tries to split it into known sections (contact, experience, education, skills), and extracts text into structured fields. Recruiters then search and filter that structured data — often by keyword. If the parser can't confidently map your content to fields, your experience effectively disappears.

## What breaks parsing

- **Multi-column layouts and tables** — text gets read out of order, or merged.
- **Headers and footers** — many parsers ignore them, so contact details vanish.
- **Images and icons** — text inside graphics is invisible to the parser.
- **Unusual section titles** — "What I've done" instead of "Experience" can be missed.

## How to write for both

Use a single-column layout, standard section headings, real text (never images), and the actual words from the job description where they're true of you. Then — because a human still makes the decision — make it readable and specific.
